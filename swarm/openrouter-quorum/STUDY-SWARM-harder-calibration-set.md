# Study-swarm dispatch — a harder, less-saturated discriminating judge-admission set

**Lever:** the #1 named lever from `eval/RESULTS.md` — only **11 of 93** admission items discriminate
among strong frontier judges, so the seat/screen margins *and* the ρ=1.0 gemma↔nvidia error-redundancy
(the thing that dropped the 3rd seat) rest on a thin signal. This is the research-grounded **design**
for fixing that. **No code** — for director review before any build.

retrieve, not recall) → 40 findings → retrieval-existence oracle (caught **4 attribution errors**,
corrected/dropped below) → **cross-family groundedness** by 2 non-Claude families (deepseek-v3.1:671b +
kimi-k2.6, reasoning-stripped). HALT actions taken: **dropped 1 mis-grounded finding** (VerifyBench
2505.15801 — both families ruled the abstract is about reference-based reward verification, not

## Research grounding (the empirical floor)

Each finding: `**claim.** Authors year (id). [verify status] → design implication.`

1. **Item discrimination follows an inverted-U in difficulty; a Fisher-information-selected subset
   out-ranks the full benchmark.** Zhou et al. 2025 (arXiv:2505.15055, PSN-IRT). [SUPPORTED] → select
   admission items by Fisher information at the *measured frontier-judge ability band*, not by raw
   maximal hardness — items far from θ_frontier carry ~zero discrimination regardless of content.
2. **Construction-time adversarial filtering re-opens a saturated gap.** Le Bras et al. 2020 (AFLite,
   arXiv:2002.04108) — drops SNLI 92%→62%, human accuracy held. [SUPPORTED] → move the saturation
   screen UPSTREAM: generate a pool, drop every item all strong judges already pass, keep the survivors.
3. **A subtle-error pair format drives even strong judges to near-random.** Tan et al. 2024 (JudgeBench,
   arXiv:2410.12784). [SUPPORTED thesis; the ~50%/64% figures are body-level] → the discriminating
   format is plausible-vs-*subtly*-wrong on an objectively-verifiable task, not surface errors.
4. **Adversarial human-and-model-in-the-loop data escapes the saturation a static set cannot.** Nie et
   al. 2020 (ANLI, arXiv:1910.14599); model-in-the-loop items are biased toward the loop model —
   Wang, Wang & Yang 2021 (arXiv:2112.08313, corrected attribution). [SUPPORTED] → treat the
   calibration set as REGENERABLE against the current frontier, generated by a DIVERSE pool (not one
   model) to avoid loop-model bias.
6. **A hard set keeps a defensible answer by separating difficulty from ambiguity.** Rein et al. 2023
   (GPQA, arXiv:2311.12022) — PhD experts 65% raw / 74% discounting their own careless mistakes;
   skilled non-experts 34% with 30+ min web. [SUPPORTED — figures in abstract] → most expert error is
   difficulty, not answer-disagreement; require an ambiguity gate so "hard" never becomes "no key."
9. **Distractors built on misleading REASONING PATHS cause up to a 45% relative F1 drop; surface cues
   are ignored.** Bhuiya et al. 2024 (arXiv:2409.05197). [SUPPORTED thesis; 45% is body-level] → make
   the "wrong" answer wrong via a plausible-but-flawed reasoning chain, not a lexical trap.
11. **Frontier models largely cannot locate the earliest erroneous step in a solution.** Zheng et al.
    2024 (ProcessBench, arXiv:2412.06559). [SUPPORTED] → add a process/reasoning-chain-validity
    construct (find-the-error-step) — a domain where judges genuinely diverge.
12. **Instruction-following judge discrimination stays hard while reward benchmarks saturate.** Wen et
    al. 2026 (IF-RewardBench, arXiv:2603.04738). [SUPPORTED thesis; Kendall figures body-level] → add a
    fine-grained instruction-following construct.
13. **RewardBench 2 exists because the original saturated (~20-pt harder).** Malik et al. 2025
    (arXiv:2506.01937). [SUPPORTED thesis] → factuality + precise-IF + math (subtle) are where harder,
    contamination-resistant items are needed as judge capability climbs.
14. **A judge can't reliably evaluate an item it couldn't answer itself.** Krumdick et al. 2025 (No Free
    Labels, arXiv:2503.05061). [thesis-only — the kappa-collapse figures were not abstract-confirmed] →
    keep items BELOW the frontier judges' own competence ceiling (so the judge CAN distinguish) yet hard
    enough to separate judges by reliability — a narrow band.
15. **Detecting a 3% model gap at 80% power needs ~969 items.** Miller 2024 (arXiv:2411.00640).
    [SUPPORTED thesis] → raw item counts must be large for fine separation…
16. **…BUT 100 IRT-curated anchor items reproduce a 14k-item MMLU score.** Polo et al. 2024
    (tinyBenchmarks, arXiv:2402.14992). [SUPPORTED] → curation-for-discrimination is what makes small-N
    work: the target is ~100+ *discriminating* anchors, not 1000 raw items (we have 11).
17. **Benchmarks saturate as the respondent-vs-judge capability gap closes.** "Benchmarks Saturate When
    The Model Gets Smarter Than The Judge" 2026 (arXiv:2601.19532). [thesis-only — title-grounded;
    abstract body not fully retrieved] → corroborates #14's ceiling.
18. **A 9-judge panel from 7 families yields only ~2 effective independent votes; three-quarters of
    nominal independence is lost to correlated errors.** Kohli 2026 (arXiv:2605.29800, corrected to
    Guneet Kohli). [SUPPORTED — the ~2-votes / three-quarters figure is in the abstract] → **this is the
    empirical mirror of our ρ=1.0 gemma↔nvidia redundancy.** Cross-family panels do NOT give independent
    votes; the only way to *measure* (and thus break) the redundancy is richer error vectors — i.e. more
    discriminating items where strong judges err *differently*. The harder set directly attacks the
    quorum blocker.

contamination 2403.07974; Wallace 2022 ~20-round dynamic collection 2110.08514; Ivey 2025 Bayesian

## The design (each choice → finding number)

A **regenerable, Fisher-info-curated, ambiguity-gated** discriminating admission set, built as a
filtered pool rather than a hand-authored list.

1. **Forward-select by Fisher information at the frontier ability band** (#1, #16). Extend the existing
   `irt.prune_items` post-hoc screen into a *forward* selection target: keep items whose difficulty `b`
   sits near the measured frontier-judge ability `θ` and whose discrimination `a` is high. Don't author
   for maximal hardness — author for `b ≈ θ_frontier`.
2. **Generate-pool → AFLite-filter → survivors** (#2, #4). Produce a large candidate pool (diverse
   generators — see #9 below), then drop every candidate ALL seated strong judges already agree on. The
   admission set is the survivor set, so it *starts* dense with discriminating items instead of 11/93.
3. **Mine seeds from cross-family judge DISAGREEMENT** (#3, #18). ai-crucible already records per-item
   judge verdicts; harvest the items where seated judges split as the candidate pool — disagreement IS
   the discrimination signal. (Re-grounded here on JudgeBench + the Kohli panel result after dropping
   the mis-grounded VerifyBench cite.)
4. **Ambiguity gate — certify a defensible single answer** (#6, #14). Every retained hard item must pass
   a dual check: (a) a defensible key exists (difficulty, not ambiguity — GPQA's 65→74% "discount own
   mistakes" pattern), and (b) the item sits BELOW the frontier judges' competence ceiling (a judge that
   can't solve it can't grade it). Omit any pair where the key can't be unequivocally certified. **This
   is the crown-jewel constraint: a harder set must not become an ambiguous set.**
5. **Add non-objective / process-validity constructs** (#11, #12, #13). The current set is all
   objective single-answer (math/logic/code-trace) → saturates. Add the domains where judges diverge at
   the frontier: reasoning-chain/process validity (find-the-earliest-error-step), subtle factuality,
   fine-grained instruction-following. NOT more arithmetic.
6. **Subtle distractors via misleading reasoning paths** (#9, #3). The "wrong" answer in a
   plausible-vs-subtly-wrong pair should be wrong via a plausible-but-flawed *reasoning chain*, not a
   surface/lexical trap — that's what separates strong judges.
7. **Size for ~100+ discriminating anchors, not 1000 raw** (#15, #16). Curation-for-discrimination is
   what makes small-N admission work; target ~100–150 *surviving discriminators* (we have 11). This also
   gives the error vectors enough mass that submodularity ρ becomes measurable instead of collapsing to
   1.0 on ~1–2 shared errors (#18).
8. **Make the set REGENERABLE, not static** (#4). Re-filter against the current frontier on a cadence
   (AFLite is a moving target); use a diverse generator pool to avoid loop-model bias. This is the
   natural on-ramp to the deferred round-against-round Designer loop.

## Proposed build (phased — for director approval)

- **Phase A (scoped, build-now-able):** the *forward* Fisher-info selection + the AFLite-style
  pool-filter over the EXISTING run's recorded judge verdicts (#1,#2,#3) + the ambiguity gate (#4).
  Produces a denser discriminating subset from material we already have — no new constructs yet.
  Directly testable: re-run the quorum panel on the filtered set and measure whether ρ-redundancy drops.
- **Phase B (new constructs — a content-authoring effort):** add the process-validity / subtle-factual
  / instruction-following construct families (#5,#6) with the ambiguity gate. This is where the set gets
  genuinely harder; it is content work + needs the gate.
- **Phase C (regeneration loop):** the AFLite re-filter cadence (#8) — the on-ramp to round-against-round
  generation (the deferred lever).
